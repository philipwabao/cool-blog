My grandmother passed away on May 21, around 3am. She was 95 years old.

She was a devout Catholic, and one of the strongest foundations her family ever
had. She believed kindness was not something you earned, but something you gave,
freely, to everyone. That was the foundation she built her life on, and the one
she leaves behind.

Her life did not begin gently. Her parents died when she was young, and she had
to work long before anyone gave her the chance to learn. She had no formal
education. But she was resilient. She taught herself to read, all on her own.
She spent her life striving for more, surviving toward the best she could make
of any situation. She carried that resilience to the very end, and she gave it
freely, the way she gave everything.

In her final years she battled dementia. There was peace between us before her
memory began slipping. Near the end, it was hard. She was still there in body,
but so much of her had already gone ahead. It was hard to watch her become a
shell of who she was. That is its own kind of grief, the kind you carry long
before the person is gone. And yet there was something gentle in it. Her
personality became childlike, and there is a peace in that. A child's mind is
the purest form of innocence there is.

My grandfather died of cancer twelve years ago, and she never recovered from
losing him. She carried that grief the way she carried everything, quietly,
turning to her prayer beads to hold what she could not say out loud. Twelve
years she waited to see him again. Wherever she is now, I hope she has. I think
she waited a long time for this. I hope she found it.

She died in her sleep, which is a fitting last chapter. She lived as kindly as a
woman of her era knew how, and she left the same way she lived. Gently.

Rest in peace, grandma.

*With love,*<br />
*Philip Abao*
